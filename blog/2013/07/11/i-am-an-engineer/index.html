I Am an Engineer

I had a job interview yesterday. I think it went rather poorly. I stumbled on the “programming” questions, which were mostly about algorithms and pattern recognition. I don’t disagree that they are important, but I think the reason I stumbled is novel.

I will be majoring in “Computer Science,” although I rarely use the scientific method. I think “Computer Science” is the intersection of maths and engineering, although the proportion depends on where you focus. In my self-taught education, I’ve picked up quite little maths. I know basic algorithmic complexity and such, but I’ve never studied algorithms in any rigor, nor have I encountered the necessity of such yet. While I’ve dabbled in the theory of computation, and can recognize the Chomsky heirarchy, I would need to pull up a book or other resource to actually use that knowledge. And so on for all the meaty, intellectual parts of “Computer Science”.

The other half of “Computer Science” is engineering, and that is what I have been focusing all my effort on the past few years. It was an implicit focus driven by necessity of implementation. Understanding space/time tradeoffs, recognizing that two slightly different implementations of an algorithm can have vastly different performance characteristics, knowing the effects of heirarchical cache, branch prediction, and so on. Designing and implementing high-performance software needs all of this knowledge to understand the tradeoffs one is making. (Of course, this is largely irrelevant when using something like CPython, which is going to trash the cache and branch predictor anyway, or PyPy where you aren’t in much control of the code generated.)
